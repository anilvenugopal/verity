AI Decision Impact Assessment

Captures the risk profile of a governed AI use case across five dimensions: decision context, data, human oversight, risks, and fairness.

Purpose

The assessment is the primary governance instrument for an intake. It answers two questions the regulator cares about: what does this AI decide, and over whom? The answers drive the computed risk tier (Limited / High / Unacceptable), which determines the approver quorum and the applicable obligation set.

All five sections are required before submission. The system computes the tier when you save — it does not wait for approval.

Decision context

Decision type
The insurance decision this AI supports. Underwriting, pricing, claims, fraud detection, and eligibility are consumer-consequential categories that raise the inherent tier (NAIC risk dimension 1).
Effect on the consumer
The worst-case outcome for a policyholder. Coverage denial and claim denial are the most severe and drive the tier to High.
EU AI Act high-risk category
An Annex III match (e.g. risk assessment & pricing in life/health insurance) floors the tier at High regardless of other answers.
Solely automated decision
Triggers GDPR Art 22 safeguards — the right to obtain human review — when there is a legal or similarly significant effect.
Affected populations
Select all that apply. Consumers and vulnerable groups raise the tier; an internal-only audience lowers it (EU Art 27 fundamental-rights impact).
Deployment scale
Production-wide deployment makes the use case material under the NAIC Model Bulletin.

Data inventory

Every data asset the use case consumes (inputs) or produces (outputs), per EU AI Act Art 10. Required fields: name, direction, data type, source, classification, personal data.

Classification
The sensitivity tier of the asset. The intake's overall classification ceiling is the most sensitive asset here.
Personal data
Special-category data (health, biometric) is the strongest signal and can raise the tier to High.
Source / provenance
Third-party and consumer-provided data carry extra due-diligence obligations (NY DFS CL-7, EU Art 10 origin).

Human oversight

How much a human can observe, intervene in, and override the system (EU AI Act Art 14). Effective oversight — an overridable control and a stop mechanism — lowers the inherent tier.

Autonomy level
Higher autonomy with weak oversight raises the tier. Fully automated + no oversight + severe consumer harm triggers auto-rejection.
Stop mechanism
EU Art 14(4)(e) requires a safe-halt for high-risk systems. Its presence, combined with override capability, makes oversight effective.

Risks

Risks to health, safety, or fundamental rights — scored likelihood × severity (EU AI Act Art 9 / ICO register). Optional, but expected for higher-tier use cases. Each risk should have a mitigation.

Fairness

Quantitative disparate-impact metrics per NY DFS CL-7 and Colorado SB21-169. Record the protected classes tested and the metrics (e.g. Adverse Impact Ratio). Document any search for a less-discriminatory alternative.
